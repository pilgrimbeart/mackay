# Materials Science and Bioinformatics

David’s “inference-first” way of thinking didn’t stop at communications and learning theory: he also applied it to the behaviour of matter and molecules.
In Cambridge he collaborated closely with materials scientists, using Bayesian neural networks to model complex metallurgical processes where the data are noisy, high-dimensional, and full of confounders—work that ranged from analysing steel plate rolling to predicting weld strength and toughness, with explicit attention to avoiding overfitting and quantifying uncertainty.
In parallel, he explored what we might now call bioinformatics: probabilistic, latent-variable “density network” models applied to protein data, treating biological sequences not as special cases but as another domain where good inference can uncover structure.
And reaching further into biophysics, he co-authored a theoretical mechanism (“ergodic pumping”) for how entropy-driven pressure forces could help drive conformational changes in biomolecular motors—explicitly proposing it as a possible design principle for nanobiotechnology.
Taken together, these strands helped to legitimise a now-familiar idea across both materials informatics and computational biology: that rigorous probabilistic modelling is not just an academic nicety, but a practical tool for extracting reliable knowledge—and better designs—from the messy complexity of the physical and living world.

In May 2015 he began working on information storage using DNA molecules, after earlier advising Nick Goldman’s team on run-length-limited (RLL) coding and its information capacity; he and Jossy Sayir worked on approaches for DNA storage based on expected error modes, and David proposed LDPC and fountain-coding techniques for practical DNA encoding.
David saw this as his most ambitious project because he thought there was a serious risk civilisation could destroy its knowledge and cultural heritage, and that encoding it in DNA could allow future generations - who might rediscover sequencing - to recover it.

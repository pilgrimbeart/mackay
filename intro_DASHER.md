# Dasher
Dasher was David’s characteristically elegant answer to a very practical question: how can a person enter text quickly and comfortably when a keyboard is slow, tiring, or impossible to use? Instead of tapping keys, Dasher turns writing into continuous steering—an on-screen “alphabet landscape” that you navigate with a mouse, touchpad, joystick, or eye-tracker, while a probabilistic language model constantly reshapes the space so likely next letters and words become easier to reach. David’s distinctive contribution was to fuse rigorous information theory and Bayesian inference with humane interface design, creating a system that is simultaneously a research demonstration and a real tool. For many users with motor impairments, Dasher offered a route to fluent communication using whatever control signal they had available; for the wider world, it helped legitimise the idea that prediction and uncertainty aren’t just engineering details, but can be made visible and helpful at the point of interaction—an influence that now shows up, in quieter ways, across modern text entry, assistive technology, and adaptive user interfaces.
